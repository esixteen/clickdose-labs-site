The Makeup Artist Took One Look at My Skin and Put Her Brushes Down.

Three weeks before my daughter's wedding, a stranger with a brush told me the truth every skincare counter had been dancing around.

By Patricia H., 58  ·  7 min read

The trial run. Taken on my phone, which turned out to be the point.

I booked the makeup trial the way you book a dentist appointment. Something to get through. My daughter's wedding was three weeks out, I had a dress, I had shoes, and I had a drawer full of skincare that cost more than both.

The artist's name was Renata. She had done, by her own count, over four hundred weddings. She tilted my chin toward the window, ran her thumb along my cheek, and then she put her brushes down.

Renata

"Before I do anything. Your makeup won't make it through the ceremony."

Me

"It never does. That's why I'm hiring you."

Renata

"No. You don't understand. It's not the makeup. Your skin is drinking it."

Drinking it. I remember laughing, because it was such an odd thing to say. And then I stopped laughing, because I knew exactly what she meant.

I call it my 2 p.m. face. Makeup on at eight, and by two it has vanished into me. Foundation settles into the lines around my mouth. Blush just... leaves. By dinner I look like I never tried, except in the creases, where I look like I tried too hard.

I thought that was a makeup problem. Four hundred weddings' worth of faces disagreed.

The Part I'd Already Paid For

Everything in my drawer had one thing in common

I told Renata what I was using. She didn't even blink, because apparently everyone her age tells her the same list.

  • The $310 counter cream. Bought after a 26-year-old in a lab coat explained my own face to me. Beautiful jar. My skin drank it by lunch, same as the cheap one.
  • The retinol that burned. Two weeks of flaking and redness before I quit. Nobody at the counter mentioned mature skin barely tolerates the stuff.
  • The drugstore tub. Honest product, honest price. It tops up moisture. It never once asked why my skin stopped holding moisture in the first place.
Renata

"None of that is going to fix it, because none of it is for you. After about 45, your skin barrier thins. The ceramides, the fats that hold it together, drop by about a third. Water gets out faster than you can put it in."

Me

"So my skin is... leaking."

Renata

"You're watering a garden with a cracked hose. And everyone keeps selling you more water."

"You're watering a garden with a cracked hose. And everyone keeps selling you more water."

I have thought about that sentence every day since. Not one counter, in forty years of buying creams, had ever put it that plainly. It was never my discipline. It was never even the products, exactly. They were fine products, formulated for a face I no longer have.

The Unglamorous Jar

What the mothers with the best skin were using

Renata reached into her kit. Past the brushes, past the setting sprays, into the pocket where she keeps what she called her "insurance."

Renata

"I do a lot of weddings. The mothers whose makeup still looks like makeup at midnight? An embarrassing number of them use this same boring jar. Started noticing it two years ago. Now I just recommend it at trials."

The jar was Maren. No pump, no gold lid, no French word for water. I turned it over expecting the usual forty-ingredient decoy list, and found three actives with their percentages printed like a receipt.

Me

"It's another cream, Renata."

Renata

"It's 88 cents a morning. Your last mistake was $310. And if your skin disagrees, they refund the empty jar. You have three weeks. What exactly is the risk?"

Three Weeks, Honestly Reported

What actually happened, week by week

I ordered it that night. Here is my diary, and I'll warn you now: it does not contain a miracle. It contains something better, which is a thing that works the way it said it would.

Days 1-10

Nothing visible, exactly as the box said there wouldn't be. But the 2 p.m. tightness eased around day four. By day ten I realized I'd stopped reapplying hand cream to my face at my desk, a habit I'd been embarrassed about for years.

Days 11-20

Did my usual makeup for a dinner. At 10 p.m. in the restaurant bathroom mirror, my foundation was still sitting on my skin instead of inside the lines around my mouth. I took a photo in the stall like a lunatic.

Day 21: the wedding

Makeup on at 9 a.m. Ceremony at 2. Crying at 2:15. Dancing until 11. Renata found me at the end of the night, looked at my face under the string lights, and just nodded, like a mechanic listening to an engine she'd fixed.

And here is the part I actually want to tell you about, because it has nothing to do with foundation.

There is a photo from that night. My daughter mid-laugh, me next to her. When I first saw it, I did the thing I have done with every photo for ten years: went straight to my own mouth, zoomed in, prepared to be disappointed.

Then I stopped. Because I was too busy looking at my daughter.

That's it. That's the whole result. The cream didn't make me 40 and it never claimed it would. It gave me back the two seconds of a photo where I used to audit my own face.
