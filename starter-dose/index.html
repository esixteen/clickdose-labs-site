Part 01

The 7 Page Blueprints

Every format below is a live page we built end to end. The anatomy is the order that works; the mistake is the one we see most. Click the live build and scroll it next to the blueprint.

Pick your blueprint

First decide: does the ad convince or does the page? That answer picks your blueprint.

1. The Story Advertorial

Cold traffic · problem-aware+

An editorial story that sells before the reader knows they're being sold.

The heaviest belief-builder there is: in our client testing, the advertorial format beat a product page by 279% on cold traffic.

Anatomy, in order

1.Label it an ad (trust up, legal happy)
2.Editorial headline + deck + byline
3.Story hook: a moment, not a pitch
4.Dialogue with an authority figure
5.Failed solutions, named honestly
6.Root cause, renamed in kitchen-table words
7.Mechanism: why this fixes the root cause
8.Product reveal, from the authority's hands
9.Honest week-by-week diary
10.Emotional payoff (not about the product)
11.Proof stats + testimonials
12.Offer + guarantee + FAQ

The killer mistake

Pitching in paragraph one. The moment it reads like an ad, the story stops working. The product should not appear until the reader has diagnosed themselves.

2. The Founder-Letter Advertorial

Skeptical audiences · burned buyers+

A first-person letter built on radical candor: what the alternatives do well, what this product won't do, the real math.

For audiences who have been burned by hype, honesty is the pattern interrupt.

Anatomy, in order

1."This is an advertisement" bar, said proudly
2.Letter headline + founder byline
3.The founder's origin moment
4.Self-diagnosis checklist ("the mirror test")
5.Honest comparison: what rivals do WELL
6.Mechanism, with doses printed
7.The anti-hype interlude (no countdown timers)
8.Honest timeline: what happens when
9.What this product will NOT do
10.Proof stats + testimonials
11.Plain-numbers offer + guarantee + FAQ

The killer mistake

Fake candor. "Our biggest flaw is we care too much" is not honesty. Admit something real (price, speed, who it's wrong for) or skip this format.

clickdoselabs.com/example/maren See the live build ↗

3. The Listicle

Cold traffic · skimmers+

"N Things Your ___ Isn't Doing." The fast, skimmable belief-builder for people who will not read a story.

Each item teaches one piece of your mechanism and quietly disqualifies an alternative.

Anatomy, in order

1.Editorial masthead (publication feel)
2.Numbered headline aimed at what they already use
3.Items 1-3: teach mechanism, disqualify rivals
4.Item 4-5: introduce the product as the fix
5.Proof block
6.Offer stack + CTA

The killer mistake

Listing features instead of teaching. Every item must make the reader smarter about their problem. Features go on the product page.

4. The Offer Page

Pre-sold traffic · post-presell+

The closer. When your ad caption or presell already did the convincing, this page has one job: take the order.

No story, maximum conversion mechanics. Never send cold traffic here.

Anatomy, in order

1.Deal bar
2.Promise hero + benefit checklist + CTA + stars
3.One hero review
4."Replaces your ___" stack
5.Mechanism strip, doses printed
6.Before / after
7.Comparison table
8.Panel stats + review wall
9.Guarantee, oversized
10.The two-options close
11.Tiered offer + FAQ

The killer mistake

Making pre-sold visitors re-read the pitch. They arrived convinced. Every paragraph of storytelling here is friction between them and the buy button.

clickdoselabs.com/example/goodboy See the live build ↗

5. The Lead Gen Page

High-intent services · urgent problems+

For offers where the conversion is a form or a call.

Speed and certainty beat cleverness: the visitor has a problem on fire and is deciding who to trust in the next ninety seconds.

Anatomy, in order

1.Loss-framed headline (what inaction costs)
2.Credibility strip (results, credentials)
3.Form or call CTA above the fold
4.Pain agitation: they know how this goes wrong
5.The 3-step process (certainty)
6.Proof: cases, numbers, faces
7.FAQ that kills the "should I wait" objection
8.Repeat CTA

The killer mistake

Asking for the form before naming the pain. A form with no empathy above it is a wall. Name their exact situation first, then ask.

clickdoselabs.com/example/harlan-reyes See the live build ↗

6. The Sales Page

Warm traffic · considered purchases+

The full argument, in one scroll.

For visitors who know you but haven't bought: build the case top to bottom and let the page do the whole job a salesperson would.

Anatomy, in order

1.Outcome-promise hero
2.The problem, told better than they'd tell it
3.Mechanism: the reason this works differently
4.Product reveal + benefit stack
5.Proof at every claim (not one proof section)
6.Offer + value framing
7.Guarantee + FAQ

The killer mistake

Burying the mechanism. Benefits without a "because" read as hype. The mechanism is what lets a skeptic believe the promise.

clickdoselabs.com/example/sable See the live build ↗

7. The Comparison Page

Solution-aware · "X vs Y" searchers+

For the buyer deciding between you and a named alternative.

The page wins by being the fairest referee in the room, then winning on the criteria that actually matter.

Anatomy, in order

1.Honest framing ("both are good, here's the real difference")
2.Criteria table, checkable claims only
3.When THEY win (yes, really)
4.When you win, with receipts
5.Switching: how easy, how fast
6.CTA matched to intent (trial, demo, migrate)

The killer mistake

Pretending the competitor has no strengths. The reader has both tabs open. One dishonest row poisons every honest one.

The AI Prompt Suite

01 The Ad Teardown View +

When to use: you found a competitor ad that's clearly working (running 60+ days) and want the machine, not the words.

You are a direct response strategist. Below is a Facebook ad (primary text + description of the creative) that has been running for [X] days, which means it's profitable.

Tear it down:
1. THE HOOK: what opens the loop in the first 2 lines, and what psychological trigger it uses.
2. THE STRUCTURE: name each beat of the caption in order (e.g. hook, failed solutions, mechanism, reveal, proof, CTA).
3. THE VISUAL: why this creative stops the scroll in THIS niche's feed, and who it filters for.
4. THE BELIEF SPLIT: does the caption do the selling (advertorial-in-caption) or does it hand off to the page? What does that tell you about the landing page's job?
5. THE STEALABLE MECHANIC: the one transferable move, stated in a single sentence that contains no words from this ad.

02 The Kitchen-Table Nickname View +

When to use: your category describes the problem in clinical or technical language, and you want the phrase customers will see in the mirror.

My product solves this problem: [DESCRIBE THE PROBLEM IN YOUR INDUSTRY'S OFFICIAL LANGUAGE].

Customers experience it like this: [DESCRIBE WHAT IT ACTUALLY LOOKS/FEELS LIKE IN DAILY LIFE].

Generate 15 "kitchen-table nicknames" for this problem: the phrase a customer would use complaining to a friend, not the term an expert would use. Rules:
- Concrete and visual (something you could point at)
- Zero jargon, zero Latin
- Sticky: once heard, they see it everywhere
- Not cute for cuteness' sake; it must DIAGNOSE

For reference quality: a skincare brand renamed "nasolabial folds" to "parentheses around my mouth." A dog brand renamed "canine hip dysplasia signs" to "the bunny hop."

Then rank your top 3 and explain what makes each one spread.
03 The Review Miner View +

When to use: before writing anything. Your customers have already written your copy; this extracts it.

Below are real customer reviews for [PRODUCT / a competitor product in my category].

Mine them:
1. PAIN LANGUAGE: exact phrases (verbatim) describing the problem before the product. Rank by frequency and emotional charge.
2. TRANSFORMATION LANGUAGE: verbatim phrases describing life after. Note which are SPECIFIC MOMENTS ("smiled in a photo") vs generic ("love it").
3. OBJECTION FOSSILS: phrases revealing what almost stopped the purchase ("I was skeptical because...").
4. THE MONEY QUOTE: the single review that could be a headline, and the rewrite of it into one.
5. NICKNAMES: any kitchen-table language customers invented for the problem or result.

Output as a swipe table I can pull from while writing.

Reviews:
[PASTE 20-50 REVIEWS]

04 The Dialogue Caption Writer View +

When to use: writing an advertorial-in-caption ad where an authority figure does the selling.

Write a Facebook ad caption as a dialogue between a skeptical customer (first person) and a trusted authority: [PICK: doctor / vet / mechanic / accountant / trainer / makeup artist / other expert who sees hundreds of cases].

Product: [PRODUCT + WHAT IT DOES]
The problem, in the customer's words: [USE YOUR KITCHEN-TABLE NICKNAME]
The mechanism (why it works): [1-2 SENTENCES]
Guarantee: [YOUR GUARANTEE]

Structure, in one-line beats (mostly dialogue):
1. Customer asks the authority about the nickname-problem
2. Authority takes it seriously; customer realizes it's not a quirk
3. Failed solutions: authority explains why what they've tried can't work
4. Root cause reframe, in plain words
5. Mechanism + product reveal FROM THE AUTHORITY (never the narrator)
6. Objection ("another [product category]?") answered with math or guarantee
7. Honest progress diary: week markers, modest early results, one emotional payoff moment that isn't about the product
8. Benefit recap + guarantee + soft CTA

Rules: one-line paragraphs. No em dashes. No hype adjectives. The authority is blunt, not salesy. The narrator is skeptical until evidence, never gushing.
05 The Framework Transplant View +

When to use: you tore down a winning ad (prompt 01) and want its machine rebuilt for your brand, in your category.

Here is the framework of a proven ad from a DIFFERENT industry:
[PASTE THE BEAT-BY-BEAT STRUCTURE FROM PROMPT 01]

Transplant it to my brand. Steal the beats, not the sentences: not one phrase from the original may survive.

My brand: [NAME + PRODUCT]
My customer: [WHO, AND WHAT THEY'VE ALREADY TRIED]
The problem nickname: [FROM PROMPT 02]
My mechanism: [WHY IT WORKS]
My proof: [STATS, REVIEWS, GUARANTEE]

Deliver:
1. The transplant map: their beat → my beat (one line each)
2. The full ad caption
3. The creative brief: one paragraph describing the image, including why it stops MY customer's scroll
4. The landing page this ad has earned: does the caption pre-sell (send to an offer page) or tease (send to an advertorial)? One sentence of reasoning.

[PASTE THE NUMBERED ANATOMY FROM THE BLUEPRINT IN THIS KIT]

Brand: [NAME]
Product + price: [DETAILS]
Customer + traffic temperature: [WHO IS LANDING HERE AND WHAT THEY KNEW BEFORE CLICKING]

07 The Headline Stack View +

When to use: you have a drafted page and a placeholder headline. Never test one headline.

Here is my landing page draft:
[PASTE PAGE OR ITS FIRST THREE SECTIONS]

Write 20 headlines for it, in 4 stacks of 5:
A. OUTCOME: the after-state, specific and time-bound where honest
B. PROBLEM-NICKNAME: leads with the kitchen-table phrase
C. MECHANISM: leads with the "because" that makes it believable
D. PATTERN-INTERRUPT: says what the reader doesn't expect from this category (honesty, anti-hype, naming the competitor)

For each stack, note which traffic temperature it fits. Then pick your champion + one challenger from DIFFERENT stacks and explain the test.
08 The Objection Autopsy View +

When to use: turning real objections into FAQ, guarantee copy, and mid-page interruptions, instead of an FAQ that answers questions nobody asked.

My product: [PRODUCT + PRICE]
My customer: [WHO]
What they've been burned by before: [PRIOR BAD PURCHASES IN THIS CATEGORY]

List the 10 real objections in the order they occur during a visit (not alphabetically). For each:
1. The objection as the customer would text it to a friend (not as a polite FAQ question)
2. WHERE it should be answered: headline, mid-page interruption, guarantee, FAQ, or price section
3. The answer, in my voice, admitting whatever is true
4. Whether the answer needs proof I may not have (flag it)

Then write the 4 strongest as an actual FAQ section, questions phrased the blunt way ("Why should I believe this ad?", "Is this a subscription trap?").
09 The Offer Stack Builder View +

When to use: structuring tiers, guarantee, and price framing for the offer section of any page.

Product: [PRODUCT], cost to me: [COGS], sells for: [PRICE], typical customer buys for [DURATION/QUANTITY].

Build my offer section:
1. TIERS: 2-3 options with anchor math (per-day or per-use cost). Mark the intended winner and say why the OTHER tiers exist (anchor, entry).
2. GUARANTEE: the strongest guarantee I can afford, named memorably (reference: "empty-bag guarantee", "bottom-of-the-bottle refund"). Include the sentence explaining WHY we can afford it.
3. PRICE FRAMING: one comparison that makes the price feel obvious (vs the expensive alternative, vs the daily habit, vs the cost of not solving it).
4. THE HONEST CLOSE: a "you have two options" section that states walking away fairly, then makes staying feel like the smart default. No fake countdown timers.
5. RISK FLAGS: anything in 1-4 that could feel scammy to a burned buyer, and the fix.
10 The Message-Match Rewrite View +

When to use: your ad and your page were written on different days by different moods, and the click can feel it.

Here is my ad:
[PASTE CAPTION + HEADLINE + CREATIVE DESCRIPTION]

Here is my landing page's first screen:
[PASTE HERO HEADLINE, SUBHEAD, CTA, AND ANY BADGES/STATS]

1. Score the message match 1-10: promise, language, emotional temperature, offer.
2. List every promise or phrase in the ad that the page never picks up.
3. List everything on the page's first screen that the clicker has no context for.
4. Rewrite the page's first screen so the click feels like the same conversation continuing: same nickname, same promise, same voice.
5. Tell me what belief-building the ad did NOT do, so I know what the rest of the page still owes the visitor.
11 The Before/After Story View +

When to use: writing proof sections (before/after, testimonials, case blurbs) that read like humans instead of stock photos with captions.

Product: [PRODUCT]. Honest timeline to results: [WHAT HAPPENS BY WHEN].
Real customer material: [PASTE REVIEWS/QUOTES/DATA, OR WRITE "ILLUSTRATIVE" IF DRAFTING PLACEHOLDERS]

Write my proof layer:
1. BEFORE/AFTER FRAMES: 2-3 paired moments, shot on a phone, of the SAME situation weeks apart. Describe each photo in one line + a one-sentence caption in the customer's voice. Specific moments, not poses ("hesitating at the bottom of the stairs" beats "sad dog").
2. SIX REVIEWS: named, located, dated. Each anchored to one concrete moment. Include exactly one 4-star review with a harmless complaint; a perfect wall of five stars reads fake.
3. ONE STAT PANEL: 3 numbers with honest labels and the disclaimer line they need.
Rules: every proof element admits the timeline honestly. If material is illustrative, add the disclosure line.

You are three different visitors landing on this page from a paid ad. For EACH persona, scroll the page and narrate where you got bored, confused, or suspicious, and where (be honest) you left:

1. THE SKEPTIC: burned twice by this category, reads fine print, hates hype.
2. THE SKIMMER: reads headlines, bolds, and captions only. Never body copy.

Then output:

- The one section to DELETE entirely

The Where It Lands Audit

Ten checks we run on any ad + page pair before a dollar of spend touches it. Score pass or fail; two or more fails means fix the page before you fix the ad.

01
The promise survives the click.The exact promise (and nickname) from the ad appears on the page's first screen, in the same words.
02
The belief split is deliberate.You can say in one sentence which half (ad or page) does the convincing. If both do it, the visitor reads the pitch twice. If neither does, nobody buys.
03
Traffic temperature matches format.Cold traffic lands on a belief-builder (advertorial, listicle). Pre-sold traffic lands on a closer. Check the blueprint chart.
04
Five-second test.A stranger can answer "what is this, who's it for, what do I get" from one screen without scrolling.
05
Every claim has proof within one scroll.A stat, review, guarantee, or mechanism sits next to each promise. Orphan claims read as hype.
06
The mechanism is stated.The page says WHY the product works, in plain words. Benefits without a because don't survive skeptics.
07
One conversion goal.One primary action, repeated. Nav links, social icons, and second offers are exits wearing costumes.
08
The guarantee kills the biggest fear.The refund terms answer the actual objection ("what if it doesn't work for ME"), and there's a reason you can afford it.
09
Mobile is the page.You checked the page on a phone, where the traffic actually is, not just on the designer's monitor.
10
You can name the test.You know what challenger this page will be tested against and what number decides. A page without a planned test is a guess with a URL.

Part 04

The Belief Split

Your ad and your page are one pitch, split in two. If you're not sure where to start, here's a simple test. Pick one of these two splits and run it first.

Split A

Long ad → short page

The caption is the advertorial: story, mechanism, proof, all before the click. The page is a pure closer (Blueprint 4): offer, guarantee, buy button.

Best when: your story is strong, your product needs explaining, and your audience reads (older demos, considered purchases).

Watch for: expensive clicks. Everyone who clicks is nearly sold, but fewer click.

Split B

Short ad → long page

The ad opens a loop (hook, nickname, curiosity) and hands off fast. The page does the convincing: advertorial or listicle first, offer second.

Best when: you want cheap clicks at volume and your page can carry the argument.

Watch for: cheap clicks that bounce. The page inherits ALL the belief-building debt.

Try this first: pick one split on purpose, write both halves in the same sitting, then test the other split against it. The brand we tore down in Ad Dose #7 hadn't touched their ad in 102 days but was on version five of the page underneath it. That's the job: the ad finds the customer, the page gets tested until it stops leaking money.
